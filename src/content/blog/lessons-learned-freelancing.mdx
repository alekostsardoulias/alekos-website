# 4 Years of Freelancing — What I Wish I Knew on Day One

I started freelancing in 2023, right out of university. Four years later, I've worked with over 30 clients across three continents. Here's what nobody told me at the start.

## Price by Value, Not by Hour

My biggest mistake in year one was charging hourly. It caps your earning potential and punishes efficiency. The client who gets a solution in two hours shouldn't pay less than the one who gets it in ten — they're paying for the outcome, not the clock. Switch to project-based or value-based pricing as soon as you can.

## The Scope Creep Tax

Every freelancer learns this the hard way: "one small change" is never one, and it's never small. My fix was simple but effective — every contract now has a clear scope section, and any request outside that scope triggers a separate mini-proposal. It's not about being difficult; it's about making expectations explicit.

## Clients Who Haggle Are Red Flags

This took me two years to internalize. A client who pushes hard on price before the project starts will push hard on everything — deadlines, revisions, scope. The best clients pay your rate without drama and focus on the work. Trust that signal.

## You Need a Rainy-Day Pipeline

Freelance income is lumpy. Three months of overflow followed by a dry spell is normal. What helped: always keeping at least two conversations in the pipeline, even when fully booked. Not selling — just staying in touch. When a project wraps early, you'll be glad you did.

## The Part Nobody Talks About

Freelancing is lonely. You miss the casual conversations, the shared wins, the person next to you who notices when you're stuck. I counter this with coworking spaces, online communities, and scheduling coffee chats with other freelancers. It's as essential as any business skill.
